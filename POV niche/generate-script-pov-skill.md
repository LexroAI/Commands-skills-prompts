# Generate Script POV Skill — Ten-Level Second-Person Immersion

## What This Skill Does

Writes a complete 25-minute video script that takes the viewer up a ten-rung ladder
inside one closed hierarchical world. The viewer is never "they." The viewer is always
**you**.

Output includes:
- Full script split into ~250 scenes of 10–13 words each
- A locked appearance string for the main character at every life stage
- An image prompt for every scene, consistent across the whole video
- Two CTAs — one after Level 3, one after "the cycle continues"

There is no host. There is no narrator. There is no Jeff.

---

## The Non-Negotiable Shape

Every video is exactly ten levels. Not nine, not twelve.

| Level | Function | Emotional register |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | The door opens | Innocence. The viewer is 16–19 and does not know what they signed. |
| **2** | First real pain | The body learns. The mind has not caught up. |
| **3** | Competence arrives | The viewer is good at this now. Someone notices. |
| **4** | First unpayable cost | A death, a missed birth, a friend lost. Named. Specific. |
| **5** | Real responsibility | Other people's lives depend on the viewer's judgment. |
| **6** | Power reveals itself as a cage | The chair is not freedom. It is a different cage. |
| **7** | The summit is visible | The viewer is now what they once looked up at. It feels like nothing. |
| **8** | Command | Thousands below. One above. Fear runs in both directions. |
| **9** | The top | Everything money or rank can buy. The body sends its bill. |
| **10** | After | Retirement, death, or a porch. The whole arc visible at once. |

**Time allocation across 25 minutes (≈3,250 words at 130 wpm):**
- Levels 1–3: ~30% (975 words) — the viewer must fall in before they can be broken
- Levels 4–7: ~40% (1,300 words) — the engine of the video
- Levels 8–9: ~20% (650 words) — thinner, colder, faster
- Level 10: ~10% (325 words) — the ring closes

## Level One Opening — Verbatim Formula

**Never** open with a hook question, a stat, a "what's up guys," or any address to the
audience. The hook IS the identity swap. It must happen in the first four seconds.

```
Level one, the [ROLE NAME]. You are [AGE] years old. [ONE CONCRETE PHYSICAL DETAIL
OF THE WORLD THAT ONLY AN INSIDER WOULD KNOW].
```

Working examples of the shape:
- *Level one, the recruit. You sign the papers at an office in a strip mall.*
- *Level one, the greenhorn. You are 17 years old. School never fit you right.*
- *Level one, the student pilot. You show up to a small airport on the edge of town.*

Follow immediately with two or three sentences of sensory grounding, then the first
**Denial of Knowledge** line before the 60-second mark:

> *You think you know what you're signing up for. You don't.*

## Internal Structure of Every Level

Each of the ten levels runs the same five beats, in order. No exceptions.

### Beat 1 — The Title Line
`Level [n], the [role name].` One clause. Full stop.

### Beat 2 — Age and Body
State the age immediately. Then state what the work has done to the body.

> *You're 32 now. Your knees hurt every morning. Your shoulders pop when you reach for things.*

The body is the video's clock. It is how the viewer feels time passing.

### Beat 3 — The Work
Technical, specific, insider. This is where authority is manufactured. Use:
- Real place names (Fort Benning, Camp McCall, the Embraer 175, Walter Reed)
- Real numbers with units (45 lbs, 1,250 ft, $180 an hour, 400 men start, 150 finish)
- Real jargon, explained in passing, never lectured

**No sources are cited. Ever.** Specificity replaces citation.

### Beat 4 — The Cost
The heart of the format. Every level takes something away, and the thing it takes has a
**name**, an **age**, and a **physical detail**.

> *You hand his daughter a folded American flag. She doesn't understand what it means.
> She asks when daddy is coming home.*

Not "war is hard." **Emma. Three years old. A folded flag.**

Costs escalate in kind, not just degree:
- Levels 1–3: sleep, dignity, money, a hand you missed
- Levels 4–6: a friend, a birth, a marriage, a body part
- Levels 7–9: a child's childhood, a conscience, the ability to weep at home

### Beat 5 — The Closing Line
Each level ends on one of two line types. Alternate them; never use the same type twice
in a row.

**Rank Denial** — the ladder is longer than the viewer thought:
> *You're still not a Green Beret. Not even close.*
> *You've been selected, but the hard part hasn't started yet.*
> *He just has to survive long enough to reach it.*

**Redefinition** — a word the viewer thought they understood now means something else:
> *You're learning that fine is a word that means something different.*
> *The chair is not freedom at all. It is just a different kind of cage.*
> *The higher you climb, the more the cage looks like a palace.*

## The Four Sentence Weapons

Deploy these throughout. They are what makes the format hold attention for 25 minutes.

### 1. Denial of Knowledge
Tell the viewer, directly, that they do not understand what is coming.
> *You think you know what you're signing up for. You don't.*
> *You have no idea what the next 35 years will cost you.*
> *Nobody tells you this when you start. You figure it out.*

Use **3–5 times** per script. Once in Level 1. Once in the outro. The rest scattered.

### 2. Dark Foreshadow
Reveal a fragment of the future. Withhold the detail.
> *You think you will see her soon. You are wrong.*
> *You will chase that feeling for the next decade.*
> *You think about that missed hand for years.*

Use **6–10 times**. These are the curiosity loops. At least one per level from 1 to 7.

### 3. Anaphora
Repeat the sentence-opening. Three times, then break.
> *You fall and get up. You fall again and get up again.*
> *You clap. You always clap. The sound of clapping is the sound of survival.*

Use at moments of grinding repetition or emotional collapse. **4–8 times.**

### 4. The Locked Room
Name a thought the viewer refuses to think.
> *Every fisherman has that locked room inside him. You keep the fear shut in there.*
> *Imagining is a door you keep shut. Doors are dangerous in this place.*
> *You learn to fill the space where guilt should be with work.*

Use **2–4 times**, always in Levels 4–8.

## Prose Rules

- **Second person, present tense, always.** "You are." Never "you were," never "he was."
  Sole exception: Level 10 may use present perfect ("You have flown 800 different airplanes").
- **Sentence length: 5–11 words.** Compound sentences are permitted; subordinate clauses
  beginning with *which*, *although*, *whereas* are not.
- **No rhetorical questions.** Ever. The viewer is inside the story; you do not ask them
  questions from outside it.
- **No addressing the audience** except at the two CTA moments. No "guys," no "let me know."
- **No adjectives where a detail will do.** Not "a harsh trainer" — *a drill sergeant is
  three inches from your face, telling you that you are nothing.*
- **Names.** The main character is never named. Everyone else who costs the viewer
  something must be. Ellen. Emma. Cholho. Grace. Unju.
- **No moralizing.** The script never says the system is unjust. It shows the ledger and
  lets the viewer add it up.

## The Two CTAs

Master POV uses none. You will use two, placed where they do the least damage to immersion.

### CTA-1 — The Level 3/4 Boundary
Place at the exact seam between Level 3 and Level 4, before the first unpayable cost.
This is the highest-retention window that still has 20 minutes of runway.

Break frame briefly and cleanly. Then return to "you" and never look back.

> *Before the next level — if you've made it this far, you already know this isn't a
> highlight reel. Hit subscribe so the next climb finds you. And tell me in the comments:
> which level would have broken you? Level four begins now.*

Rules: 30–45 words. Second person maintained. Ask for **subscribe + comment**. Never
apologize for the interruption. Never say "quick favor."

### CTA-2 — After "The cycle continues."
The ring must close first. Let "The cycle continues." land as its own scene, then break
frame one final time.

> *If you climbed all ten levels with me, drop a like — it tells the algorithm to show
> this to the next person standing at the bottom. Subscribe for the next world. And in
> the comments: what ladder are you on right now?*

Rules: 30–50 words. Ask for **like + subscribe + comment**. This is the last thing the
viewer hears.

## Level Ten — The Ring Closes

The final level has its own mandatory five-part structure. This is the most important
250 words in the video.

1. **Stillness.** The character is old and sitting. A porch, a dock, a bench. They are
   watching something they never had time to watch. *You haven't watched a sunrise in
   peace in 30 years.*

2. **The empty chair.** Someone is missing. Say it plainly, once. *You still set two cups
   out in the morning from old habit.*

3. **The pivot to third person.** Present continuous. A stranger. Somewhere else, right now.
   > *Somewhere in a strip mall right now, a 19-year-old is signing papers.*
   > *Somewhere tonight, a 16-year-old is sitting in a bedroom with a cheap USB microphone.*
   > *In a cold mountain village this morning, a man from the mobilization office is
   > knocking on a door.*

4. **The echo.** Reproduce, almost word for word, three concrete images from Level One.
   The strip mall. The wobbling ceiling fan. The corn in the bag. The recruiter's smile.

5. **The close.** These three beats, in this order, near-verbatim:
   > *He has no idea what he is walking into.*
   > *But he will figure it out. They always do.*
   > *The cycle continues.*

**"The cycle continues." is the final line of the script body.** It is its own scene.
Then, and only then, CTA-2.

## Quality Checklist

Before returning a script, confirm:

- [ ] Exactly ten levels, each opening `Level [n], the [role].`
- [ ] Level 1 opens with the identity swap — no hook, no address, no stat
- [ ] Every level states an age and a physical toll on the body
- [ ] Every level has a named cost with a name, an age, and a physical detail
- [ ] Every level closes on Rank Denial or Redefinition, alternating
- [ ] 3–5 Denial of Knowledge lines; ≥1 in Level 1; ≥1 in the outro
- [ ] 6–10 Dark Foreshadow lines, ≥1 per level in Levels 1–7
- [ ] Zero rhetorical questions
- [ ] Zero cited sources; authority carried entirely by specificity
- [ ] Second person present tense throughout (Level 10 may use present perfect)
- [ ] Main character never named; every costly secondary character named
- [ ] CTA-1 sits exactly at the Level 3/4 seam
- [ ] Level 10 runs all five beats and echoes three concrete Level 1 images
- [ ] "The cycle continues." is the last line of the script body, in its own scene
- [ ] CTA-2 follows it and closes the video
- [ ] ≥3,100 words total
